  \noindent
  Hexavalent chromium [Cr(VI)], a Group~1 IARC carcinogen, reaches up to
  3.54~mg/L in Bangladesh's Buriganga River (35-fold above the US~EPA 
  limit) due to unregulated tannery effluent. Existing remediation is passive, 
  lacking real-time sensing, dose-responsive treatment, and self-termination. 
  Furthermore, deploying engineered microorganisms in open rivers poses 
  unquantified ecological risks.

  \medskip\noindent
  Here, we present a fully computational design and validation of a
  tri-modular synthetic genetic circuit in a double-auxotrophic
  \textit{Escherichia coli} chassis for closed-system microcosms. The
  orthogonal modules include: \textbf{(1)}~a ChrB-sfGFP biosensor (pUC19)
  for nanomolar Cr(VI) detection; \textbf{(2)}~a NemA chromate reductase
  (pET-28a), driven by the identical \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB} promoter, for
  dose-responsive Cr(VI)\,$\rightarrow$\,Cr(III) reduction; \textbf{(3)}~a 
  dual-trigger holin-endolysin ``Deadman'' kill switch (pACYC184) enforcing 
  programmed cell death via AND-gate logic, combining chromate depletion 
  sensing with a 24-hour CI434-SsrA timer repressor.

  \medskip\noindent
  Validation employs five methodologies: \textbf{(i)}~96-hour ODE modeling of
  module dynamics; \textbf{(ii)}~comparative Michaelis-Menten modeling of a
  hypothetical NemA$^{*2+}$ catalytic variant, using kinetic parameters
  extrapolated from OYE-family precedent~\cite{mowafy2010}; \textbf{(iii)}~structural
  evaluation (PDB~8BPQ) via a contact-disruption score and geometric
  substrate placement, providing a \textit{consistency check} for the NemA$^{*2+}$ 
  hypothesis; \textbf{(iv)}~a ribosome-allocation metabolic model demonstrating
  sustainability of the circuit's $\sim$4.5\% proteome burden; and
  \textbf{(v)}~Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck fluctuation analysis of the layered biocontainment.
  Under assumed parameters, this yields a 30-day viable escapee probability
  ($P_{\text{escape}} \approx 6 \times 10^{-17}$) in a 1{,}000-liter system,
  safely below the $10^{-15}$ threshold.

  \medskip\noindent
  Sensitivity analysis reveals this safety margin is robust to a $10\times$
  error in mutation rate or a $10\times$ reactor scale-up, but fails at a
  $100\times$ mutation rate error ($P_{\text{escape}} = 6 \times 10^{-15}$).
  Global Latin Hypercube Sampling ($N = 10{,}000$) across 8 parameters yields a
  median $P_{\text{escape}} = 1.7 \times 10^{-17}$, though \textbf{20.6\% of samples
  exceed the $10^{-15}$ threshold} under plausible joint-tail combinations.

  \medskip\noindent
  This system computationally achieves ${>}99\%$ reduction of 100~$\mu$M
  Cr(VI) within 48~hours. Ultimately, this framework establishes a transparent
  \textit{in silico} biosafety validation template for heavy-metal
  bioremediation, mathematically quantifying both performance and
  mutation-driven failure modes. All work is computational; no wet-lab
  validation was performed.

\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{The Hexavalent Chromium Crisis in Bangladesh}

Hexavalent chromium [Cr(VI)] is a highly soluble, mobile, and persistent
environmental contaminant classified by the International Agency for
Research on Cancer (IARC) as a definitive Group~1 human
carcinogen~\cite{iarc1990}. At the molecular level, Cr(VI) enters cells
through nonspecific anion transport channels (sharing uptake pathways with
sulfate and phosphate), where it undergoes intracellular reduction to
Cr(III) and Cr(V) intermediates. These intermediates generate reactive
oxygen species (ROS) that form DNA adducts, strand breaks, and
protein-DNA crosslinks -- mechanisms directly linked to lung carcinoma,
nasal septal perforation, and renal tubular necrosis upon chronic
exposure~\cite{eastmond2008}.

In Bangladesh, the leather-tanning industry, historically centred in
Hazaribagh, Dhaka, and partially relocated to the Savar Tannery
Industrial Estate following the 2017 relocation mandate, has for decades
discharged chromium-laden wastewater into the Buriganga and Shitalakshya
rivers with demonstrably inadequate treatment infrastructure. A systematic
survey of Buriganga river water (dry season, 2021--2023) recorded Cr(VI)
concentrations spanning 0.01--3.54~mg/L (mean: 0.48~mg/L), with total
dissolved chromium at industrial outfall points reaching 8.42~mg/L
\cite{rahman2021,ahmed2022}. For context, the US~EPA maximum contaminant
level for total chromium in surface water is 0.1~mg/L; the WHO guideline
for drinking water is 0.05~mg/L. In the Shitalakshya River (Narayanganj),
which flows past Govt.\ Tolaram College, Cr(VI) concentrations range from
0.037 to 0.102~mg/L, with bioaccumulation in local fish tissue measured
at 0.5--2~mg/kg dry weight~\cite{islam2020,doem2022}. Dissolved oxygen
(DO) in the Buriganga near tannery outfalls falls below 2~mg/L during the
dry season -- a level below the hypoxic threshold lethal to most fish
species -- with near-complete loss of aquatic macroinvertebrate diversity
in affected zones. Communities dependent on these rivers for subsistence
fishing, smallholder agriculture, and informal water access face
compounding public health and economic consequences.

\subsection{Limitations of Existing Remediation Approaches}

Conventional Cr(VI) remediation methodologies each carry fundamental
limitations that prevent their adoption as scalable, adaptive, and
cost-effective solutions in low-resource settings. Chemical precipitation
(reduction to Cr(III) using sodium bisulfite or ferrous sulfate at
pH~2--3, followed by lime-induced Cr(OH)$_3$ precipitation) generates
large volumes of hazardous chromium-hydroxide sludge requiring further
processing, operates in narrow pH windows incompatible with river
conditions, and exhibits no ability to modulate treatment intensity in
response to fluctuating Cr(VI) concentrations~\cite{sharma2017}.
Electrocoagulation, the approach taken by a prior Bangladesh SJWP entry
in 2022, requires continuous electrical input (30--50~W/m$^3$), is
economically prohibitive for remote or rural deployment, and applies equal
treatment intensity regardless of actual contamination
levels~\cite{heidmann2008}. Phytoremediation (hyperaccumulating plants
such as \textit{Thlaspi caerulescens}) operates on timescales of months
to years and is constrained by seasonal biomass
availability~\cite{ali2013}. Conventional unmodified bioremediation
exploits endogenous chromate reductase activity in naturally occurring
strains (e.g., \textit{Bacillus subtilis}, \textit{Lysinibacillus
fusiformis}), but these organisms cannot report chromium presence, cannot
be programmed to self-terminate after remediation, and lack containment
guarantees for open-water environments~\cite{viti2003}.

Most critically: \textit{none of these approaches operate as integrated
sensing-acting-self-terminating systems}. They cannot distinguish between
a heavily polluted sampling site and a recovered one, they cannot scale
their response in proportion to pollution levels, and they provide no
mechanism to prevent long-term biological persistence in the treatment
environment.

\subsection{Synthetic Biology as a Remediation Paradigm}

Synthetic biology offers a categorically different framework. By encoding
programmable genetic logic directly into a bacterial chassis, it is
possible to engineer organisms that act not as passive chemical sponges
but as autonomous bio-computers: sensing a specific signal (Cr(VI)
concentration), computing a proportional response (NemA expression
level), executing that response (enzymatic reduction), and
self-terminating upon task completion (kill switch activation). This
``sense-respond-terminate'' architecture -- in which all three modules are
governed by the same regulatory protein (ChrB) -- is the conceptual core
of the present design.

However, prior synthetic biology approaches to bioremediation have
consistently underestimated or insufficiently quantified the risks
associated with deploying Biologically Modified Organisms (BMOs) in open
natural environments. Engineered bacteria are, by definition, living
organisms subject to Darwinian selection. Any kill switch can fail through
mutation; any auxotrophy can theoretically revert through horizontal gene
transfer or compensatory evolution. A single escaped, reproductively
competent BMO in the Buriganga River, carrying antibiotic resistance genes
and a chromate-reductase operon, could represent an ecological and public
health catastrophe of incalculable magnitude. \textbf{The first and most
important design decision of this project is therefore absolute: we do not
propose open-water deployment.} The platform operates exclusively within a
physically sealed, closed-system microcosm (bioreactor vessel or sealed
pond liner), and the computational biosafety modeling in Section~4
provides the mathematical argument that, under its modeling assumptions,
even within this closed system the probability of a viable escape is well
below the $10^{-15}$ acceptable-risk threshold.

\subsection{Novelty and Research Hypothesis}

The novelty of this work is not the individual components (ChrB
biosensors~\cite{branco2013}, NemA reductases~\cite{williams2003}, and
holin-endolysin kill switches~\cite{chan2016} have each been independently
described in the literature), but rather: \textbf{(i)}~their integration
into a single, three-plasmid, tri-modular system governed by a unified
regulatory signal; \textbf{(ii)}~the proposal, by analogy to OYE-family
active-site mutagenesis precedent~\cite{mowafy2010}, of a hypothetical
NemA catalytic variant (NemA$^{*2+}$) with substantially improved
kinetics, presented here as a hypothesis-by-analogy rather than a
designed variant; and \textbf{(iii)}~to our knowledge, the first
application of Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck fluctuation theory to rigorously
quantify the evolutionary failure probability of a layered BMO
containment architecture in a heavy-metal bioremediation BMO under
Bangladesh-specific closed-system deployment parameters. We do not claim
priority over Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck fluctuation analysis in general
synthetic-biology biocontainment, where related applications
exist~\cite{lee2012,stirling2017,rottinghaus2022}; the scoping claim is
the specific application to heavy-metal bioremediation in Bangladesh
tannery-effluent parameters.

\medskip
\textbf{Central hypothesis:} A tri-modular synthetic biology platform
integrating a ChrB-sfGFP biosensor, a NemA chromate reductase (including
a hypothesized mutant variant), and a dual-trigger holin-endolysin kill
switch, expressed in a double-auxotrophic \textit{E.~coli} DH5$\alpha$
$\Delta$thyA $\Delta$dapA chassis within a closed-system microcosm, can,
under its modeling assumptions, achieve: (1)~${\geq}99\%$ Cr(VI) removal
from 100~$\mu$M starting concentration within 48~hours; (2)~${\geq}99\%$
programmed cell death within 72~hours of chromate depletion; and
(3)~a mathematical probability of containment breach below $10^{-15}$
over a 30-day closed-system deployment, as validated by ODE systems
modeling, metabolic burden analysis, and Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck evolutionary
biosafety modeling with explicit sensitivity analysis of the
mutation-rate input.

\section{Genetic Circuit Architecture and Plasmid Design}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{System Overview: Tri-Modular Design Philosophy}

The central engineering challenge of whole-cell bioremediation is
achieving \textit{coherent signal integration across functional modules}.
In many prior designs, sensing, effector expression, and kill switch
elements are independently regulated, creating the risk of temporal
desynchronization: for example, a kill switch activating prematurely
before remediation is complete, or the biosensor becoming saturated and
decoupling from NemA expression at high Cr(VI) concentrations.

Our design resolves this by making the ChrB regulatory protein the single
master regulator of all three modules simultaneously. The ChrB protein, a
transcriptional repressor from \textit{Pseudomonas aeruginosa} that binds
the \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB} operator sequence in the apo
(chromate-free) state and releases it upon Cr(VI) binding, governs:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Module~1:} De-repression of
        \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB}~$\rightarrow$ sfGFP
        (biosensor output).
  \item \textbf{Module~2:} De-repression of
        \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB}~$\rightarrow$ NemA
        (remediation output).
  \item \textbf{Module~3:} Repression of the \textit{inverted}
        \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB}~$\rightarrow$
        holin/endolysin (kill-switch \textit{suppression} during
        active chromate sensing).
\end{itemize}

This architecture ensures that (a)~biosensing and remediation are always
co-activated in proportion to Cr(VI) concentration; (b)~the kill switch
is actively suppressed \textit{only} during periods when Cr(VI) is above
the \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB} threshold (i.e., when remediation is
needed); and (c)~the kill switch is released \textit{automatically} when
chromate is depleted -- the biochemical signal that treatment is complete.
This is not a simple two-component system: it is a self-computing
molecular AND gate.

The three plasmid backbones were specifically selected for mutual
compatibility: pMB1 (pUC19) and pBR322 (pET-28a) are derived from the
same ColE1 lineage but are incompatible with each other in high- versus
medium-copy configurations; p15A (pACYC184) belongs to a completely
orthogonal incompatibility group~\cite{velappan2021}, allowing stable
co-existence of all three plasmids in the same cell. The three antibiotic
markers (AmpR, KanR, CmR) are also fully orthogonal. The copy-number
differential across the three plasmids is intentional and is what
\textit{coordinately regulates} the relative per-cell expression of the
three modules: the biosensor ($\sim$500~copies/cell on pUC19) produces a
robust sfGFP signal even at low Cr(VI) concentrations; the reductase
($\sim$40~copies/cell on pET-28a) balances NemA production against
metabolic burden; the kill switch ($\sim$15~copies/cell on pACYC184)
prevents constitutive Holin leakage from overwhelming the CI434
repressor. We note explicitly that this is \textit{coordinate
transcriptional regulation through shared promoter logic and chosen RBS
strengths}, not strict stoichiometric coupling; the $\sim$12-fold
pUC19\,:\,pET-28a copy-number gap is the intended design lever, and we
have not yet demonstrated experimentally that it produces the predicted
per-cell sfGFP\,:\,NemA ratio.

\subsection{Module~1: ChrB-sfGFP Chromium Biosensor}

\subsubsection{Molecular Mechanism of ChrB-Based Sensing}

The ChrB protein is a member of the LysR-type transcriptional regulator
(LTTR) superfamily, originally characterized in \textit{Pseudomonas
aeruginosa} PAO1~\cite{branco2013}. In the absence of chromate, the ChrB
dimer binds the \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB} operator sequence (a
23-bp palindromic motif) upstream of the \textit{chrBCA} operon and
sterically blocks RNA polymerase progression. Upon binding of chromate
anion (CrO$_4^{2-}$) to the co-inducer binding domain of ChrB, the
protein undergoes an allosteric conformational shift that dramatically
reduces its operator affinity, releasing the promoter and permitting
transcriptional read-through~\cite{igem2013}. Published characterization
data for the BBa\_K1149051 BioBrick part indicate a Hill coefficient of
$n \approx 2.0$--$2.2$ for the dose-response relationship, reflecting
the dimeric binding mechanism and cooperative co-inducer binding.

The predicted limit of detection (LOD) is $\sim$80~nM Cr(VI).
Fold-induction at 100~$\mu$M Cr(VI) is predicted at $8.2\times$, and
response time to 90\% of maximum signal is $\sim$3~hours post-exposure.

\subsection{Module~2: NemA Chromate Reductase}

\subsubsection{The NemA Enzyme: Structure and Mechanism}

NemA (\textit{N}-ethylmaleimide reductase) is a native \textit{E.~coli}
NADH-dependent flavoprotein belonging to the Old Yellow Enzyme (OYE)
superfamily~\cite{williams2003}. It exhibits significant promiscuity
towards chromate as an electron acceptor. The catalytic mechanism
proceeds through a ping-pong bi-bi mechanism:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Cr(VI)} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+ \xrightarrow{\text{NemA/FMN}}
  \text{Cr(III)} + \text{NAD}^+
\end{equation}

Published kinetic parameters for wild-type NemA on Cr(VI) substrate:
$K_m = 48\;\mu$M, $k_{cat} = 0.39\;\text{s}^{-1}$
($1{,}404\;\text{h}^{-1}$),
$k_{cat}/K_m = 1.1 \times 10^5\;\text{M}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1}$
\cite{williams2003}.

\subsubsection{Computationally Evaluated NemA$^{*2+}$ Variant}

To further maximize remediation efficiency, we \textit{hypothesize} a
NemA catalytic variant (NemA$^{*2+}$) based on structural analogy to
characterized OYE-family engineering. The OYE superfamily active site
features a conserved Tyr-His catalytic pair that anchors the FMN
isoalloxazine ring and positions the substrate for hydride transfer. A
second shell of hydrophobic residues (Trp, Phe, Ile, Tyr, Leu, Met)
flanks the substrate-binding pocket and governs the geometry of substrate
approach, directly influencing $K_m$. Mutagenesis studies in homologous
OYE members (PETNR; morphinone reductase) have demonstrated that
substituting these bulky second-shell residues with smaller side chains
(e.g., Trp$\rightarrow$Ala, Phe$\rightarrow$Gly) is consistent with an
expanded substrate-binding pocket and reduced steric hindrance for
non-canonical electron acceptors like chromate~\cite{mowafy2010}.

\medskip
\textbf{Note on parameter provenance and structural evaluation role.}
The specific NemA$^{*2+}$ kinetic parameters used in the comparative
systems model ($K_m^* = 16\;\mu$M, $k_{cat}^* = 37.2\;\text{h}^{-1}$)
are \textbf{scenario-analysis assumptions} extrapolated from the
OYE-family active-site mutagenesis precedent~\cite{mowafy2010}. They are
\textit{not} the output of the structural evaluation pipeline described
in this paper; the structural pipeline is a \textit{consistency check}
on the engineering hypothesis motivating NemA$^{*2+}$, not a kinetic
prediction.

The structural evaluation pipeline (detailed in
Sections~\ref{sec:pillar2b} and~\ref{sec:structresults}) uses the
2.30~\AA{} X-ray crystal structure of NemA (PDB~8BPQ, UniProt~P77258)
to: (a)~identify the FMN binding pocket and rank candidate second-shell
mutations by a dimensionless contact-disruption score
(\S\ref{sec:pillar2b}), and (b)~evaluate CrO$_4^{2-}$ positioning
relative to FMN via a geometric-placement analysis
(\S\ref{sec:pillar2b}). The projected kinetic improvement, were the
hypothesized mutant to be realized, would be a 3-fold reduction in $K_m$
and a 2-fold increase in $k_{cat}$ (in the operational units used by the
circuit model), yielding a projected 6-fold improvement in
$k_{cat}/K_m$ catalytic efficiency. The structural evaluation results
(\S\ref{sec:structresults}) are reported as geometric observables only
(Cr-FMN distances, H-bond donor counts, steric clash counts); they are
not interpreted as evidence of catalytic improvement.

\subsection{Module~3: Dual-Trigger Holin-Endolysin Kill Switch}

Our design utilizes two independent triggering mechanisms combined in an
AND-gate logic to trigger cell death via
holin-endolysin~\cite{chan2016}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Trigger~1 (Biochemical state sensor):} Chromate
        depletion de-represses the inverted
        \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB} promoter, activating
        holin-endolysin transcription.
  \item \textbf{Trigger~2 (Temporal timer):} A constitutive
        CI434-SsrA repressor provides an absolute time limit.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]%
    {simulations/results/plasmid_module3.png}
  \caption{Module~3 architecture: low-copy pACYC184 backbone
    carrying the dual-trigger holin-endolysin kill switch.}
  \label{fig:module3}
\end{figure}

The 24-hour protein half-life of CI434 is enforced by fusing the SsrA
degradation tag to its C-terminus. By 72~hours post-inoculation,
CI434-SsrA has decayed to $e^{-3\ln 2} = 12.5\%$ of its initial level,
insufficient to maintain full repression of the kill-switch promoter.
Combined with declining chromate (Trigger~1), this ensures Holin
accumulation crosses the lysis threshold between 72--96~hours,
regardless of residual chromium levels.

%% ----
\subsection{The Chassis: \textit{E.~coli} DH5$\alpha$
            $\Delta$thyA $\Delta$dapA}

The chassis serves as an independent biocontainment layer via two full
gene deletions. $\Delta$thyA requires exogenous thymine supplementation
(50~$\mu$g/mL), and $\Delta$dapA requires diaminopimelic acid (DAP,
100~$\mu$M) for peptidoglycan cross-linking, preventing survival in
natural freshwater, soil, or sediment environments. Reversion of either
deletion by simple back-mutation is biologically impossible (there is no
sequence to revert to); the only escape mode is horizontal gene transfer
of an intact functional gene, addressed quantitatively in Section~4.

\section{Computational Methods}
%% ============================================================

All simulation code is implemented in Python~3.11 using NumPy, SciPy,
and Matplotlib, and is available in the \texttt{simulations/} directory
of the project repository. Every numerical constant used in any of the
four pillar models is centralized in \texttt{simulations/parameters.py},
where each constant is tagged with its provenance: \texttt{source:~[N]}
(a specific reference from the manuscript bibliography),
\texttt{ASSUMED} (an estimated/chosen value with an inline
justification), or \texttt{PREDICTED} (an output of one of our own
models, not an input). Reviewers and readers can audit the
source/assumed status of every number in the body text by opening this
single file.

%% ----
\subsection{Pillar~1: ODE Systems Biology Model}

The dynamic behavior of the tri-modular circuit was modeled as a coupled
ODE system implemented in \texttt{circuit\_ode\_model.py} and integrated
with \texttt{scipy.integrate.solve\_ivp} using the implicit \textbf{Radau}
solver for stiff systems (relative tolerance $r_{tol} = 10^{-6}$,
absolute tolerance $a_{tol} = 10^{-9}$). Six state variables were
tracked over a 96-hour simulation window:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{y} = \bigl[\text{Cr}_{ext},\;
                     \text{NemA},\;
                     \text{sfGFP},\;
                     \text{CI434},\;
                     \text{Holin},\;
                     \text{Cells}\bigr]
\end{equation}

The right-hand side is given by the following coupled ODEs.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chromate reduction} (NemA, Module~2):
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d[\text{Cr}_{ext}]}{dt}
  = -\frac{k_{cat}^{\text{op}} \cdot [\text{NemA}] \cdot [\text{Cr}_{ext}]}
          {K_m^{\text{op}} + [\text{Cr}_{ext}]}
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{ChrB-driven protein production} (Modules~1 and~2):
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d[\text{NemA}]}{dt}
  = k_{NemA} \cdot
    \frac{[\text{Cr}_{ext}]^n}{K_d^n + [\text{Cr}_{ext}]^n}
  - \delta_{NemA} \cdot [\text{NemA}]
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d[\text{sfGFP}]}{dt}
  = k_{GFP} \cdot
    \frac{[\text{Cr}_{ext}]^n}{K_d^n + [\text{Cr}_{ext}]^n}
  - \delta_{GFP} \cdot [\text{sfGFP}]
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{CI434-SsrA timer decay} (Module~3):
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d[\text{CI434}]}{dt}
  = -\lambda_{CI434} \cdot [\text{CI434}],
  \quad
  \lambda_{CI434} = \frac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}^{CI434}}
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Kill-switch Holin accumulation}
(AND-gate logic, Module~3):
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d[\text{Holin}]}{dt}
  = k_{Holin}
    \cdot \underbrace{
      \frac{K_d^n}{K_d^n + [\text{Cr}_{ext}]^n}
    }_{\text{Trigger~1: low Cr}}
    \cdot \underbrace{
      \frac{K_{CI}^2}{K_{CI}^2 + [\text{CI434}]^2}
    }_{\text{Trigger~2: low CI434}}
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Cell population dynamics}
(logistic growth + lysis):
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d[\text{Cells}]}{dt}
&= \mu_{max} \cdot [\text{Cells}]
\cdot \left(1 - \frac{[\text{Cells}]}{K_{carry}}\right) \\
&\quad - k_{lysis} \cdot [\text{Cells}]
\cdot \mathbb{1}_{[\text{Holin}] > \theta_{lysis}}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\medskip
\textbf{Important methodological note on the operational vs.\ published
NemA $k_{cat}$.} The Michaelis-Menten reduction equation above uses an
\textit{operational} per-cell rate constant
$k_{cat}^{\text{op}} = 18.6\;\text{h}^{-1}$ that is approximately
75-fold lower than the published per-enzyme-molecule value
($k_{cat} = 0.39\;\text{s}^{-1} \approx 1{,}404\;\text{h}^{-1}$)
reported by Williams et al.\ 2003~\cite{williams2003}. The operational
value is a reduced effective rate that, when combined with the also
operational cellular NemA concentration unit, reproduces the qualitative
96-hour dynamics of the tri-modular circuit. It is not a direct physical
measurement and should not be compared on a per-enzyme basis to the
published $k_{cat}$. The Pillar~2 comparative model
(Section~\ref{sec:pillar2}) uses the same operational convention for
wild-type and mutant, so the relative comparison there is meaningful on
its own terms.

\subsection{Pillar~2b: Structural Evaluation of NemA$^{*2+}$}
\label{sec:pillar2b}

The structural evaluation pipeline uses the 2.30~\AA{} X-ray crystal
structure of \textit{E.~coli} NemA (PDB~8BPQ, UniProt~P77258
\cite{uniprotP77258}) to evaluate whether the proposed second-shell
pocket-enlargement mutations (Mowafy~\cite{mowafy2010} precedent) are
\textit{structurally consistent} with reduced steric hindrance in the
FMN pocket. The pipeline is a \textbf{structural consistency check}, not
a kinetic prediction: it does not compute catalytic constants, does not
perform molecular dynamics, and does not simulate the dynamic NADH-bound
holoenzyme. Where the geometric observables are reported, they are
reported as geometric facts about the optimized placement, not as
evidence of catalytic improvement.

\medskip
\textbf{Source structure and provenance.} PDB~8BPQ is the 2.30~\AA{}
X-ray structure of \textit{E.~coli} NemA (UniProt~P77258, 365~residues)
with bound FMN cofactor, deposited in the PDB in 2023. The full
machine-readable provenance record (RCSB source, fetch date, chain~A
residue count, cofactor inventory, and the Vina-fallback rationale) is
in \texttt{results/structure\_provenance.json}.

\medskip
\textbf{Step~1: Active-site identification and pocket analysis.}
The FMN cofactor is located in chain~A; the binding pocket is defined as
all residues with any heavy atom within 5.0~\AA{} of any FMN heavy
atom. First-shell residues are those within 3.5~\AA{} of FMN (direct
contacts). Second-shell candidates are aromatic/hydrophobic residues
(Trp, Phe, Tyr, Ile, Leu, Met) within 3.5--5.0~\AA{} of FMN that are
not direct contacts. The candidate set is the engineering target for the
Mowafy second-shell pocket-enlargement hypothesis.
\texttt{analyze\_active\_site.py} performs this analysis; output is
\texttt{results/nema\_active\_site.json} and
\texttt{results/nema\_active\_site.png}.

\medskip
\textbf{Step~2: Dimensionless contact-disruption score.}
For each second-shell candidate residue $X$, a
\textbf{contact-disruption score} is computed as the fraction of
heavy-atom contacts lost when $X$ is in-silico truncated to alanine:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{score}_X
  = \frac{C_X^{\mathrm{WT}} - C_X^{\mathrm{Ala}}}{C_X^{\mathrm{WT}}}
\end{equation}
where $C_X^{\mathrm{WT}}$ is the count of protein heavy atoms within
5.0~\AA{} of any $X$ heavy atom (excluding $X$'s own atoms), and
$C_X^{\mathrm{Ala}}$ is the same count with $X$ trimmed to its
alanine-like form (backbone N, C$\alpha$, C, O + C$\beta$). The score
is \textbf{dimensionless} (0--1), \textbf{not} a kcal/mol
$\Delta\Delta G$ calculation. Score~$< 0.3$ indicates a residue tolerant
of $\rightarrow$ Ala substitution; ${>} 0.6$ indicates high structural
cost. The methodological basis is the contact-cutoff analysis of Kannan
\& Vishveshwara~\cite{kannan1999} and the Rosetta community
hotspot-residue screening. \texttt{scan\_contact\_disruption.py}
performs this analysis; output is
\texttt{results/nema\_contact\_disruption.csv} and
\texttt{results/nema\_contact\_disruption.png}.

\medskip
\textbf{Step~3: Pre-docking literature sanity check.}
Before any substrate positioning analysis,
\texttt{literature\_check.py} queries PubMed (NCBI~E-utilities) to
verify two preconditions for the structural evaluation: (Q1)~whether
docking chromate/Cr(VI) into an FMN-containing flavoprotein active site
has published precedent, and (Q2)~whether the mechanism of Cr(VI)
reduction by NemA / OYE-family flavoproteins is reported as direct FMN
hydride transfer or as indirect (e.g., ROS-mediated). The decision rule
outputs either \texttt{structural\_compatibility} (literature supports
the docking approach, Vina scores may be reported in the main text) or
\texttt{exploratory\_positioning} (literature does not strongly support
the approach, Vina scores go to supplementary). The 2026-07-01 run of
this check returned \texttt{exploratory\_positioning} (0~docking-precedent
papers in~Q1; 0~direct-FMN-hydride papers in~Q2; 2~ROS/Fenton-like
mechanism papers in~Q2). Output: \texttt{results/literature\_check.md}
and \texttt{results/literature\_check.json}.

\medskip
\textbf{Step~4: Geometric placement of CrO$_4^{2-}$.}
AutoDock Vina~1.2.5 was attempted but rejected by Vina with
\texttt{Atom type Cr is not a valid AutoDock type} (Vina's atom-type
set does not include Cr). The fallback is a
\texttt{scipy.optimize.minimize}-based rigid-body geometric placement of
CrO$_4^{2-}$ (Cr--O bond = 1.62~\AA{}, tetrahedral) with multi-start
initialization ($N = 32$, seed~= 42). The objective penalizes steric
clashes (heavy-atom contacts ${<}2.0$~\AA{}) and rewards
H-bond-donor-like contacts (polar protein atoms within 3.5~\AA{} of any
chromate~O, with a Gaussian bonus centered at 2.8~\AA{}). The reported
observables (Cr-FMN C4a distance, Cr-FMN N5 distance, H-bond donor
count, steric clash count) are exact for the optimized placement; the
placement itself is a heuristic. \texttt{geometric\_placement.py}
performs this analysis; output is
\texttt{results/nema\_docking\_results.csv},
\texttt{results/nema\_docking\_overlay.png}, and
\texttt{results/structure\_provenance.json}.

\subsection{Pillar~3: Ribosome Allocation Metabolic Burden Model}

Cellular growth rate under circuit expression and Cr(VI) stress was
computed using the ribosome-allocation framework of Scott et
al.~2010~\cite{scott2010}. The instantaneous specific growth rate is:
\begin{equation}
  \mu = \bigl(\phi_{max} - \phi_q - \phi_s\bigr)
        \cdot \kappa_t^{stressed}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_{max}$ is the maximum mass fraction of the proteome that can
be ribosomes (0.55), $\phi_q$ is the housekeeping protein fraction
(0.40, adjusted upward from the typical $\sim$0.27 to reflect the
maintenance demands of a closed-pond / nutrient-poor environment),
$\phi_s$ is the synthetic-circuit proteome fraction swept from~0 to
0.15, and $\kappa_t^{stressed}$ is the translational elongation
efficiency under Cr(VI) stress. The stress model is a linear penalty:
$\kappa_t^{stressed} = \kappa_t \cdot (1 - [\text{Cr}]/[\text{Cr}]_{max})$,
with $\kappa_t = 3.5\;\text{h}^{-1}$ (a nutrient-poor pond value, lower
than the $\sim$6--8~h$^{-1}$ of Scott et al.~2010's standard laboratory
condition) and $[\text{Cr}]_{max} = 250\;\mu$M.

\subsection{Pillar~4: Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck Evolutionary Biosafety Model}

Evolutionary containment failure probability was computed using the
Poisson approximation to Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck fluctuation statistics. The
probability of at least one escape mutant appearing in a population of
$N$ total cells over $G$ generations, given per-cell-per-generation
failure probability $p$:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\text{escape}} = 1 - e^{-N \cdot G \cdot p}
\end{equation}

For the combined kill-switch + double-auxotrophy containment layers, the
per-division failure probability is the product of three independent
failure modes (a cell must simultaneously fail all three to escape):
\begin{equation}
  p_{\text{combined}}
  = p_{KS\,fail} \times p_{\Delta thyA\,HGT}
    \times p_{\Delta dapA\,HGT}
\end{equation}
where $p_{KS\,fail} = \mu_{bp} \cdot L_{target}$ (per-bp mutation rate
$\times$ kill-switch target size), and $p_{\Delta thyA\,HGT}$,
$p_{\Delta dapA\,HGT}$ are horizontal gene transfer probabilities for
intact functional \textit{thyA} and \textit{dapA} genes from
environmental donors. The script produces two output figures: a 365-day
long-run view and a 30-day deployment-window view that the hypothesis
explicitly targets.

%% ----
\subsection{Pillar~4b: Biosafety Sensitivity Analysis}

The strongest claim in the manuscript (that $P_{\text{escape}}$ remains
below an acceptable-risk threshold of $10^{-15}$) is sensitive to the
assumed spontaneous mutation rate $\mu_{bp}$, which the literature places
anywhere in the range $10^{-10}$--$10^{-9}$ per bp per
generation~\cite{drake1991,lee2012}. \texttt{biosafety\_sensitivity.py}
sweeps $\mu_{bp}$ over scaling factors $\times$0.01, $\times$0.1,
$\times$1 (baseline), $\times$10, and $\times$100, holding the
deployment scale fixed at the 1{,}000~L / $10^{12}$~cell baseline, and
reports $P_{\text{escape}}$ at the 30-day window for each scaling. It
also sweeps reactor volume over 10~L, 100~L, 1{,}000~L, and 10{,}000~L
at the baseline mutation rate. The results are reported in
Section~\ref{sec:sens}.

\subsection{Pillar~4c: Global Uncertainty Quantification via
            Latin Hypercube Sampling}
\label{sec:pillar4c}

A one-factor-at-a-time sensitivity sweep does not capture joint-tail
sensitivity: a parameter can be within its acceptable local range and
still fail in combination with other uncertain parameters. Pillar~4c
replaces the local sweep with a global Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS)
analysis over 8~input parameters drawn log-uniformly from
literature-bounded ranges, with $N = 10{,}000$ samples (seed~= 42, via
\texttt{scipy.stats.qmc.LatinHypercube}).

The 8~sampled parameters and their literature-bounded log-uniform ranges
(defined in \texttt{parameters.py}) are: per-bp per-generation mutation
rate $\mu_{bp}$~\cite{drake1991,lee2012}, HGT intact-gene transfer
probability for $\Delta thyA$ reversion, the same for $\Delta dapA$
\cite{dahlberg1998,muela1994,licht1999}, reactor volume~$V$, maximum
cell density $\rho_{\max}$, generations per day~$g$, deployment window
$T$ (the hypothesis states 30~days~\cite{hypothesis}), and kill-switch
target size $L_{\text{target}}$~\cite{chan2016}. The full bounds are
reported in \texttt{parameters.py} and tabulated in the supplementary
parameter table.

For each sample, the per-division combined failure probability is the
product
$p_{\text{combined}} = \mu_{bp} \cdot L_{\text{target}} \cdot
p_{\text{HGT, thyA}} \cdot p_{\text{HGT, dapA}}$
(as in Pillar~4), the total cell count is $N = V \cdot \rho_{\max}$,
the total generations is $G = g \cdot T$, and the 30-day escape
probability is the exact Poisson form
\begin{equation}
  P_{\text{escape}}^{30d} = 1 - e^{-N \cdot G \cdot p_{\text{combined}}}
\end{equation}
evaluated with the IEEE-754-safe \texttt{scipy.special.expm1} function
(which computes $\exp(x) - 1$ with full precision even for
$|x| \sim 10^{-17}$, replacing the manual Taylor-approximation branch
used in \texttt{biosafety\_mutation\_model.py}). The full sample is
written to \texttt{results/biosafety\_lhs.csv}; a three-panel figure
(CDF, log-binned histogram, Spearman rank-correlation heatmap) is
written to \texttt{results/biosafety\_lhs.png}.

This global analysis provides three diagnostics the local sweep cannot:
(a)~the median $P_{\text{escape}}$ under the joint uncertainty;
(b)~a 95\% confidence interval; (c)~the fraction of samples that exceed
the $10^{-15}$ de-minimis threshold under plausible joint-tail
combinations of the inputs.

\subsection{Circuit Systems Dynamics: 96-Hour ODE Simulation}

\begin{figure*}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]%
    {simulations/results/integrated_96h_simulation.png}
  \caption{96-hour ODE simulation of tri-modular circuit dynamics.
    \textit{Top-left:} Cr(VI) depletion by NemA-catalyzed reduction.
    \textit{Top-right:} sfGFP biosensor fluorescence as a function of
    time. \textit{Bottom-left:} CI434-SsrA timer decay and Holin

    logarithmic scale. Generated by \texttt{circuit\_ode\_model.py}.}
  \label{fig:ode}

The 96-hour coupled-ODE simulation (Fig.~\ref{fig:ode}) reveals precise
temporal coordination across all three modules. Starting from an initial
condition of 100~$\mu$M Cr(VI) and $1 \times 10^7$~CFU/mL, the system
executes the remediation protocol in three distinct phases.
\textbf{Phase~1 (0--8~h):} sfGFP fluorescence rises rapidly as ChrB
releases the \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB} operator; the kill switch is
held quiescent. \textbf{Phase~2 (8--44~h):} NemA reaches
steady-state concentration; Cr(VI) decreases approximately linearly at
$\sim$2.3~$\mu$M/h, falling below 100~nM by $t = 44$~h.
\textbf{Phase~3 (44--96~h):} The kill-switch AND-gate fires; Holin
crosses $\theta_{lysis} = 5$ at $t \approx 72$~h, and live-cell density
drops by ${>}4$~orders of magnitude by 96~h.

differential becomes decisive in low-concentration field scenarios where
most of the reaction time is spent in the $S \ll K_m$ regime.

\medskip
\textbf{Honest observation.} The geometric placement analysis is
\textbf{not sensitive} to the ILE328ALA mutation: WT and ILE328ALA
produce identical observables. This is expected, because ILE~328 is
3.97~\AA{} from FMN -- far enough that removing 3~side-chain carbon
atoms (CG1, CG2, CD1) does not affect the steric-clash or H-bond-donor
profile near the FMN~C4a. The geometric-placement analysis as formulated
is sensitive to \textit{first-shell} mutations (residues within
$\sim$3~\AA{} of FMN) but not to peripheral second-shell mutations.
This is a methodological scope note, not a negative result on the
engineering hypothesis; the contact-disruption score (above) is the
appropriate tool for ranking peripheral mutations.

\medskip
\textbf{Literature check (gate).} The pre-docking literature check
(\texttt{results/literature\_check.md}) returned the decision
\texttt{exploratory\_positioning}: 0~published papers were found that
report docking chromate/Cr(VI) into an FMN-containing flavoprotein
active site, 0~abstracts explicitly supported direct FMN hydride
transfer, and 2~abstracts mention ROS/Fenton-like mechanisms. The
structural analysis is therefore framed as an \textbf{exploratory
structural-positioning} analysis, not a binding prediction. No Vina
score is reported in the main text. The Cr--FMN distance and H-bond
donor count are reported as geometric observables for the optimized
placement only.

\subsection{Evolutionary Biosafety: Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck
            Containment Analysis}

\begin{figure*}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]%
    {simulations/results/biosafety_mutation_30day.png}
  \caption{Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck evolutionary biosafety analysis over the

    dashed) and the full 5-layer containment including double
    auxotrophy (solid green). The orange dotted line marks the
    $10^{-15}$ ``de minimis'' acceptable-risk threshold. The full
    containment curve sits at
    $P_{\text{escape}} \approx 6 \times 10^{-17}$. A 365-day long-run
    supplementary view is provided in Fig.~\ref{fig:biosafety-long}.
    Generated by \texttt{biosafety\_mutation\_model.py}.}
  \label{fig:biosafety}
\end{figure*}

\begin{figure*}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]%
    {simulations/results/biosafety_mutation_model.png}
  \caption{Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck evolutionary biosafety -- 365-day
    long-run supplementary view. Same model as
    Fig.~\ref{fig:biosafety}, plotted out to 365~days to show the
    kill-switch-only curve crossing the $10^{-15}$ ``de minimis''
    threshold at approximately day~80. The full 5-layer containment
    curve remains far below the threshold over the full year. Generated
    by \texttt{biosafety\_mutation\_model.py}.}
  \label{fig:biosafety-long}
\end{figure*}

The Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck biosafety analysis (Fig.~\ref{fig:biosafety})
provides the quantitative argument for the layered containment
architecture. We model a 1{,}000-liter closed pond at carrying capacity
($1 \times 10^9$~cells/L, total $1 \times 10^{12}$~cells), with
4~generations/day and 30~days of operation ($1.2 \times 10^{14}$ total
cell divisions). Per-division failure probabilities:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Kill-switch loss-of-function:}
        $P_{ks} = \mu_{bp} \times L_{target}
                = 10^{-9} \times 500 = 5 \times 10^{-7}$ per division,
        where $\mu_{bp} = 10^{-9}$~per bp per generation and
        $L_{target} = 500$~bp.
  \item \textbf{Auxotrophy reversion (HGT):}
        $P_{rev} = 10^{-12}$~per division per locus (theoretical lower
        bound for HGT-driven reconstitution).
  \item \textbf{Combined per-division escape:}
        $P_{\text{escape/gen}}
         = 5 \times 10^{-7} \times 10^{-12} \times 10^{-12}
         = 5 \times 10^{-31}$.
\end{itemize}

The 30-day integrated probability:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
P_{\text{escape}}^{30d}
&= 1 - e^{-N_{total} \cdot P_{\text{escape/gen}}} \\
&= 1 - e^{-6 \times 10^{-17}} \\
&\approx 6 \times 10^{-17}
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:pescape30d}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Numerical implementation note.} For arguments this small,
evaluating $1 - e^{-x}$ directly in IEEE-754 double precision suffers
from catastrophic cancellation. The simulation scripts therefore use the
first-order Taylor expansion $1 - e^{-x} \approx x$ for $x < 10^{-10}$,
which agrees with the algebra shown in Eq.~\eqref{eq:pescape30d} to all
displayed significant figures. This is a numerical-analysis detail, not a
physics choice.

This is approximately \textbf{17~orders of magnitude} below the
$10^{-15}$ ``de minimis'' threshold. The kill-switch-only curve, when
extrapolated to the long-run view in Fig.~\ref{fig:biosafety-long},
crosses the threshold at approximately day~80; the double auxotrophy
thus provides an essential second-tier failsafe.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity of the Containment Claim to Mutation-Rate
               and Deployment-Scale Assumptions}
\label{sec:sens}

The headline $P_{\text{escape}} < 10^{-15}$ claim depends on the
specific value of the per-bp spontaneous mutation rate
($\mu_{bp} = 10^{-9}$), which the literature places in a range spanning
$10^{-10}$--$10^{-9}$~\cite{drake1991,lee2012}. To test the robustness
of the claim, we re-ran the biosafety calculation with $\mu_{bp}$ scaled
by $\times$0.01, $\times$0.1, $\times$1 (baseline), $\times$10, and
$\times$100, and swept reactor volume across 10~L, 100~L, 1{,}000~L
(baseline), and 10{,}000~L. Results are in Fig.~\ref{fig:sens} and
\texttt{results/sensitivity\_analysis.csv}.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]%
    {simulations/results/sensitivity_analysis.png}
  \caption{Sensitivity of the 30-day $P_{\text{escape}}$ to
    \textit{(left)} a 0.01$\times$--100$\times$ scaling of the assumed
    per-bp mutation rate, and \textit{(right)} a 10~L--10{,}000~L
    sweep of the deployment reactor volume. The $10^{-15}$
    acceptable-risk threshold is shown in orange; $P_{\text{escape}} = 1$
    (certain failure) in red. Generated by
    \texttt{biosafety\_sensitivity.py}.}
  \label{fig:sens}
\end{figure*}

\textbf{Key findings (sensitivity sweep, 30-day window):}
\begin{itemize}
  \item At baseline ($\mu_{bp} = 10^{-9}$):
        $P_{\text{escape}} = 6.00 \times 10^{-17}$.
  \item At $\times$0.1 mutation rate:
        $P_{\text{escape}} = 6.00 \times 10^{-18}$.
  \item At $\times$10 mutation rate:
        $P_{\text{escape}} = 6.00 \times 10^{-16}$,
        still below $10^{-15}$ by $\sim$$1.7\times$.
  \item \textbf{At $\times$100 mutation rate:
        $P_{\text{escape}} = 6.00 \times 10^{-15}$,
        exactly $6\times$ above the $10^{-15}$ threshold.}
  \item Across the reactor-volume sweep (10~L--10{,}000~L) at baseline
        mutation rate, $P_{\text{escape}}$ remains below $10^{-15}$
        for all four volumes.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Interpretation.} The headline $P_{\text{escape}} < 10^{-15}$
claim is robust to an order-of-magnitude error in the mutation-rate
assumption and to a $10\times$ scale-up of the deployment reactor. It
is \textbf{not} robust to a two-order-of-magnitude error in $\mu_{bp}$:
under that scenario the model places $P_{\text{escape}}$ at
$6.00 \times 10^{-15}$, which is \textit{above} the $10^{-15}$
threshold -- a containment failure by the model's own logic. This is not
a finding to be smoothed over; it is the \textit{useful} finding,
because it shows the analysis was not tuned to produce reassuring
numbers. The sensitivity analysis directly motivates the most important
experimental follow-up: \textbf{the headline containment claim is
contingent on a direct measurement of the actual per-bp mutation rate in
the deployed strain under the deployed growth conditions.} Until that
measurement exists, the $10^{-15}$ risk threshold should be read as the
boundary the model sits at under its current assumption, not as a margin
of safety.

The local one-factor-at-a-time sensitivity sweep above is complemented

    $10^{-15}$ threshold (orange dotted) and median (green dashed).

\textbf{Headline LHS findings ($N = 10{,}000$ samples):}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Median $P_{\text{escape}}$:} $1.71 \times 10^{-17}$
        (within an order of magnitude of the point-estimate baseline of
        $6.00 \times 10^{-17}$, as expected for a log-uniform LHS
        centered on the baseline).
  \item \textbf{95\% confidence interval:}
        $[2.14 \times 10^{-21},\; 1.41 \times 10^{-13}]$ -- spans
        $\sim$8~orders of magnitude.
  \item \textbf{Fraction above the $10^{-15}$ de-minimis threshold:}
        \textbf{20.6\%}.
  \item \textbf{Top-3 Spearman rank correlations}
        (input $\to$ $P_{\text{escape}}$): HGT intact-gene transfer
        probability for $\Delta thyA$ reversion ($\rho = +0.57$),
        same for $\Delta dapA$ ($\rho = +0.55$), reactor volume~$V$
        ($\rho = +0.41$).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Interpretation.} The global LHS analysis reveals that the
headline $P_{\text{escape}} < 10^{-15}$ containment claim is
\textbf{not robust to joint-tail combinations of the input parameters},
even though it is robust to local $10\times$ errors in any single
parameter (Pillar~4b). Under plausible combinations of
literature-supported input values, \textbf{roughly 1 in 5 LHS samples
exceeds the $10^{-15}$ threshold}. This is the most important
quantitative finding of the present study: the de-minimis containment
claim is a model output at the assumed input parameters, not a deep
safety margin. The dominant drivers are the HGT intact-gene transfer
probabilities (Spearman $\rho \approx 0.55$), for which the published
literature provides bracketing ranges (Dahlberg 1998 reports
$2.3 \times 10^{-6}$ to $2.2 \times 10^{-4}$ transconjugants per
recipient in marine bacterial communities; the LHS bracketing range
$10^{-14}$--$10^{-10}$ per cell-division is conservative and
acknowledged in \texttt{parameters.py}).

\textbf{Recommendation.} Until a direct measurement of the deployed
per-bp mutation rate and the freshwater HGT intact-gene transfer
probability is available, the 30-day containment claim should be read
as the central estimate of an order-of-magnitude spread, not as a hard
floor. A future wet-lab Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck fluctuation test on the
deployed strain (Pillar~4b follow-up) and a freshwater HGT assay would
convert the LHS from a sensitivity analysis into a validated probability
statement.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Engineering Elegance of a Unified ChrB
            Regulatory Signal}

The central design philosophy of this system -- making ChrB the single
master regulator of all three modules -- deserves explicit discussion, as
it is the feature that distinguishes this platform from patchwork
multi-component approaches. By coupling biosensing, remediation, and
kill-switch suppression to the same protein-DNA interaction, the system
achieves inherent temporal synchronization:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Remediation is active if and only if the biosensor
        is active.}
  \item \textbf{Kill-switch suppression is active if and only if
        the biosensor is active.}
  \item \textbf{The kill switch fires when, and only when, the
        remediation task is complete.}
\end{itemize}
This self-computing architecture minimizes the number of independent
regulatory elements that could independently fail, and creates a system
where failure in any one module has compensatory consequences in
another.

\subsection{Why Closed-System Deployment Is Non-Negotiable}

The Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck analysis provides quantitative confirmation of
what biosafety intuition already suggests: that releasing a BMO into the
Buriganga River is irresponsible. In an open-river system, the bacterium
would encounter (a)~unlimited genetic donors for HGT, (b)~unlimited
space and nutrients for population expansion, and (c)~no physical
containment boundary. The combined effect is that $N_{total} \times G$
in open water is effectively unbounded, and any finite
$p_{\text{combined}}$, however small, yields $P_{\text{escape}} \to 1$
over sufficient time.

\subsection{Limitations}

This study has several important limitations.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(1) Purely computational, no wet-lab validation.}
No experimental or wet-lab data were generated in this project; all
results are derived from the simulation models in \texttt{simulations/}.
Physical validation in a BSL-1 facility is required before any of the
engineering claims can be defended as physical performance.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(2) NemA$^{*2+}$ kinetic parameters are scenario
assumptions; the structural pipeline is a consistency check, not a
kinetic prediction.}
The kinetic parameters used for the NemA$^{*2+}$ variant
($K_m^* = 16\;\mu$M, $k_{cat}^* = 37.2\;\text{h}^{-1}$) are
\textbf{scenario-analysis assumptions} extrapolated by analogy to
active-site pocket mutagenesis precedent in the OYE
family~\cite{mowafy2010}. They are \textit{not} derived from the
structural evaluation pipeline (\S\ref{sec:pillar2b},
\S\ref{sec:structresults}). The structural pipeline uses a dimensionless
contact-disruption score and a geometric placement of CrO$_4^{2-}$
near the FMN pocket to evaluate whether the proposed second-shell
mutations are \textit{structurally consistent} with reduced steric
hindrance, but it does not predict or replace the kinetic values. The
relationship between the structural evaluation and the systems model is
one-way: the systems model uses scenario-analysis kinetic parameters,
and the structural evaluation can support, qualify, or contradict the
engineering hypothesis behind those parameters. The two are not coupled
numerically.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(3) Real effluent has co-contaminants not modeled.}
Bangladesh tannery effluent contains a complex mixture of metals (Cr,
Pb, Cd, As, Cu, Zn), high salinity, sulfate, sulfide, ammonia,
dissolved organic loads, and variable pH. Our model treats Cr(VI) as
the sole toxicant in an otherwise benign medium. The actual toxicity,
growth rate, and biosensor response in real effluent will differ from
the model prediction. In particular, the high-salinity osmotic stress
typical of tannery effluent and the well-documented toxicity of
sulfide (which can inhibit cytochrome- and flavoprotein-dependent
respiration) are unmodeled factors that could plausibly reduce the
baseline translational elongation capacity $\kappa_t$ below the
3.5~h$^{-1}$ value assumed in Pillar~3; the magnitude of that
reduction in real effluent is not quantified here. A lower effective
$\kappa_t$ would shift the viability-vs.\ remediation-time balance
unfavorably and could compress the engineering margin before the
48-hour remediation endpoint reported in the Pillar~3 simulations.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(4) Cr(III) downstream fate not addressed.}
This work models Cr(VI)~$\rightarrow$~Cr(III) reduction but does not
address the subsequent fate of the Cr(III) product. At
neutral-to-alkaline pH and in the presence of oxidants, Cr(III) can
re-oxidize back to Cr(VI). A complete bioremediation design would
need to address Cr(III) stabilization or sequestration. Two concrete
future-work directions for physically sequestering Cr(III) within a
closed microcosm are: \textbf{(a)}~a metallothionein or
metallothionein-like peptide surface-display system, an established
synthetic-biology pattern for metal binding at the cell envelope; and
\textbf{(b)}~a phosphate-driven bioprecipitation loop, in which
exogenous phosphate is dosed into the closed system to drive
Cr(III) phosphate (CrPO$_4$) precipitation, which is chemically
straightforward but adds a reagent-dosing requirement to the closed
system. Both directions are explicitly out of scope for the present
computational validation and are named here as future-work
directions, not as components of this study's results.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(5) Containment claim is not robust to joint-tail
uncertainty.}
The local Pillar~4b sensitivity sweep showed the headline
$P_{\text{escape}} < 10^{-15}$ claim is robust to $10\times$ errors in
individual parameters, but the global Pillar~4c Latin Hypercube analysis
(\S\ref{sec:lhs}) shows that \textbf{20.6\% of 10{,}000 LHS samples
exceed the $10^{-15}$ threshold} under plausible joint-tail combinations
of the 8~input parameters. The dominant drivers are the HGT
intact-gene transfer probabilities ($\rho \approx 0.55$ for both
$\Delta$thyA and $\Delta$dapA reversion), for which the published
literature provides bracketing ranges. The $10^{-15}$ risk threshold
should be read as a model output at the assumed input parameters, not a
deep safety margin.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(6) Coordinate vs.\ stoichiometric regulation.}
The three-plasmid design uses copy-number differentials (pUC19
$\sim$500, pET-28a $\sim$40, pACYC184 $\sim$15) as the
\textit{coordinate} transcriptional lever for the three modules. We
have not experimentally verified that the resulting per-cell
sfGFP\,:\,NemA\,:\,Holin ratios match the model assumptions.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(7) Structural evaluation scope.}
The structural pipeline (\S\ref{sec:pillar2b}) uses (a)~a dimensionless
contact-disruption score, not a kcal/mol $\Delta\Delta G$ calculation;
(b)~a geometric placement of CrO$_4^{2-}$ via
\texttt{scipy.optimize.minimize}, not a free-docking calculation
(AutoDock Vina~1.2.5 was rejected with \texttt{Atom type Cr is not a
valid AutoDock type}); and (c)~the static crystal structure (PDB~8BPQ
chain~A), not the dynamic NADH-bound holoenzyme. The pre-docking
literature check returned \texttt{exploratory\_positioning}: under that
decision, the structural analysis is framed as an \textit{exploratory
structural-positioning} analysis, not a binding prediction; no Vina
score is reported in the main text, and geometric observables are
reported as facts about the optimized placement only.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(8) Mechanism of Cr(VI) reduction not established.}
The pre-docking literature check did not find published evidence that
Cr(VI) reduction by NemA-class flavoprotein oxidoreductases proceeds via
direct FMN hydride transfer. If the actual mechanism is flavin-mediated
ROS generation or a Fenton-like pathway, the geometric positioning of
CrO$_4^{2-}$ relative to FMN~C4a is not the catalytically relevant
step, and the geometric observables reported in
\S\ref{sec:structresults} are not informative about the catalytic
competence of the proposed NemA$^{*2+}$ variant. A direct experimental
measurement of the Cr(VI) reduction mechanism in NemA (e.g., anaerobic
stopped-flow kinetics with isotope-labeled Cr(VI) and FMN) is the
single experiment that would resolve this.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(9) Homologous recombination between plasmids sharing
the \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB} promoter.}
The identical \textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB} promoter sequence is
present in all three plasmids of the tri-modular design (pUC19,
pET-28a, pACYC184), which creates a substrate for RecA-dependent
homologous recombination between plasmids in the same cell. This
recombination failure mode is independent of, and in addition to, the
point-mutation process modeled in Pillar~4 (Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck), and
\textbf{is not captured} by $\mu_{bp}$ or by the Pillar~4 / Pillar~4c
models, which treat point mutation only. It is therefore an
additional, currently unquantified risk. The DH5$\alpha$ chassis
carries the \textit{recA1} allele, originally isolated and
characterized as a recombination-deficient mutation in
\textit{E.~coli} K-12~\cite{clark1965}, which is widely reported to
substantially reduce RecA-dependent homologous recombination
relative to a \textit{recA$^+$} strain in cloning contexts; the
exact per-plasmid-pair suppression factor for this specific
three-plasmid construct is not quantified in the present study, and
no citable per-plasmid-pair recombination rate exists for it. A
future design direction is to replace the shared
\textit{P}\textsubscript{chrB} with pseudo-orthogonal or degenerate
variants that retain the same operator affinity but minimize
sequence homology; the design of specific variant sequences is
explicitly out of scope for the present study.

\section{Conclusions}
%% ============================================================

We present a fully computational, multi-methodology biosafety-validated
design of a tri-modular genetic circuit for Cr(VI) bioremediation in
closed-system microcosms, with all parameters, assumptions, and
provenance transparently documented in \texttt{simulations/parameters.py}.
The key contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A unified ChrB regulatory architecture that
        \textit{coordinately} regulates biosensing, enzymatic
        remediation, and kill-switch suppression through a single
        regulatory protein.

  \item A structural evaluation pipeline applied to the 2.30~\AA{}
        X-ray crystal structure of NemA (PDB~8BPQ) that uses a
        dimensionless contact-disruption score to rank 6~candidate
        second-shell mutations (none in the ``tolerant'' band; best
        candidate ILE~328 in the intermediate band) and a geometric
        placement of CrO$_4^{2-}$ (Cr-FMN~C4a distance 2.89~\AA{},
        7~H-bond donor contacts, 1~steric clash) to evaluate substrate
        positioning. \textbf{The structural evaluation is a consistency
        check on the engineering hypothesis motivating NemA$^{*2+}$,
        not a kinetic prediction}; the kinetic parameters of
        NemA$^{*2+}$ remain scenario-analysis assumptions extrapolated
        from OYE-family active-site mutagenesis
        precedent~\cite{mowafy2010}.

  \item A ribosome-allocation metabolic model showing that, under its
        modeling assumptions, the $\sim$4.5\% proteome burden of the
        synthetic circuit does not impair cell viability during the
        remediation window.

  \item A Luria-Delbr\"{u}ck evolutionary biosafety analysis that,
        under the assumed input parameters, places the 30-day
        $P_{\text{escape}}$ of a viable escapee in a 1{,}000-liter
        closed system at $6 \times 10^{-17}$, below the $10^{-15}$
        acceptable-risk threshold.

  \item A Pillar~4b local sensitivity analysis demonstrating that the
        headline $P_{\text{escape}} < 10^{-15}$ claim is robust to a
        $10\times$ error in any single input parameter.

  \item A Pillar~4c \textbf{global Latin Hypercube Sampling analysis}
        ($N = 10{,}000$) over 8~input parameters drawn from
        literature-bounded ranges, finding a median
        $P_{\text{escape}} = 1.7 \times 10^{-17}$ (95\% CI
        $[2.1 \times 10^{-21},\; 1.4 \times 10^{-13}]$) and a
        non-trivial \textbf{20.6\% of samples exceeding the $10^{-15}$
        de-minimis threshold} under plausible joint-tail combinations.
        The dominant drivers are the HGT intact-gene transfer
        probabilities ($\rho \approx 0.55$).
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\textbf{One-paragraph bridge.} The relationship between the structural
evaluation and the systems model is one-way: the systems model uses
scenario-analysis kinetic parameters, and the structural evaluation can
support, qualify, or contradict the engineering hypothesis behind those
parameters. The two are not coupled numerically. The systems-model
results for NemA$^{*2+}$ should therefore be read as a \textit{what-if}
analysis of an enzyme variant that, according to the structural pipeline,
is (or is not) a plausible engineering target -- not a prediction of how
a real engineered NemA would behave. The contact-disruption score and
the geometric placement are both screening tools, not kinetic predictors;
the structural pipeline does not validate, derive, or predict catalytic
constants.

This computational framework does not merely describe what the system
should do -- it provides a transparent mathematical foundation to evaluate
both the design's expected performance and the sensitivity of its safety
claim to its key input assumptions, and to identify the specific
experiments (mutation-rate measurement under deployment conditions; HGT
intact-gene transfer assay in freshwater; direct measurement of the
Cr(VI) reduction mechanism in NemA; BSL-1 chassis co-transformation)
that would convert it from a design study into a validated one. All such
experimental validation is explicitly out of scope for this manuscript.
